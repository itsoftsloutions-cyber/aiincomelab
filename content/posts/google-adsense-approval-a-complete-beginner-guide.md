Google AdSense approval feels mysterious until you understand what the review team is actually checking. It is not random, and it is not about tricks. It is about whether your site looks like a legitimate, useful publication that a real person built — not a content farm set up to extract ad revenue.

This guide covers what Google actually evaluates, what gets sites rejected, and the fastest legitimate path to approval.

---

## What Google actually reviews

AdSense reviewers (and their automated systems) check for two things: **policy compliance** and **site quality**. Both must pass.

### Policy compliance checklist

- **Privacy Policy page** — required. Must be linked from your footer and contain standard language about data collection.
- **About page** — tells Google (and readers) who runs the site and why. A real person or team, not a generic "we are a content company."
- **Contact page** — a working contact method (email or form). Google wants to know they can reach you.
- **No prohibited content** — hate speech, adult content, dangerous products, counterfeit goods. This is an automatic rejection.
- **Owned or licensed images** — do not use Google Images screenshots as your blog photos.

### Site quality signals

- **Original content** — posts must be primarily your own writing, not copied from elsewhere.
- **Sufficient content** — there is no published minimum, but reviewers historically flag sites with fewer than 10-15 posts. Aim for 15-20 posts of 700+ words each before applying.
- **Real navigation** — header navigation, footer links, working internal links. A site that only has blog posts with no structure looks incomplete.
- **Mobile-friendly design** — most traffic is mobile. AdSense ads need to display correctly on phones.
- **Fast load times** — not a formal requirement, but a slow site hurts user experience signals that factor into quality assessment.

## What gets sites rejected (and why)

Understanding rejections saves weeks of guesswork.

**Thin content:** Posts under 300 words or that repeat the same generic information across every page. If your site has 20 posts that all say roughly the same thing in different words, that reads as a content farm.

**AI-generated content without original value:** Pure AI output with no first-hand experience, no specific examples, no opinions — exactly what Google's Helpful Content system penalizes. This is the most common failure point for new AI bloggers in 2026. The fix: add your own perspective, specific observations, and real examples to every post.

**Incomplete or broken site:** Construction notices, placeholder text, missing pages, broken links. Your site should look finished before you apply.

**Copyrighted images:** Using random images from the web without rights is a quick rejection. Use Unsplash, Pexels, or AI-generated images instead.

**Insufficient original content:** If your entire site could have been written by someone who has never left their desk and has no experience with the topics they are writing about, it will struggle.

## The fastest path to approval

This is a realistic timeline for a new blog:

| Week | Action |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Set up site, add Privacy Policy, About, and Contact pages |
| 2-4 | Publish 15-20 posts of 700-1,200 words each |
| 4-5 | Check mobile display, test all links, ensure fast load time |
| 5 | Apply for AdSense |
| 6-7 | Receive decision (usually 1-14 days) |

**The content quality bar:** Each post should answer a specific question completely. Imagine someone searches for your title and lands on your post — do they leave with the information they needed, or do they need to go read another site? If they get everything they need from you, that is a quality post.

## How to apply step by step

1. Go to **adsense.google.com** and sign in with your Google account.
2. Click "Get started" and enter your site URL.
3. Connect your site by adding the AdSense code snippet to your site's `<head>` tag. Most website builders have a field for this in site settings.
4. Fill in your payment information and country.
5. Submit and wait for review — typically 1-14 days.

**If you are rejected:** Google will give you a reason code. The most common ones and their fixes:

| Rejection reason | Fix |
| --- | --- |
| Insufficient content | Add more posts; improve existing post depth |
| Site doesn't comply with policies | Check Privacy Policy, About, Contact pages |
| Site is down or inaccessible | Check your domain is live and indexed by Google |
| Valuable inventory under construction | Remove any placeholder or "coming soon" sections |

## What to do immediately after approval

Getting approved is just the beginning. Optimizing your ad placement matters as much as getting approved.

**Auto ads vs manual ad units:** Start with Auto ads — Google places ads automatically in positions it predicts will earn the most. Once you have enough traffic data (usually 30 days), experiment with manual placements.

**Ad placements that work well for blogs:**
- In-article ads: between sections of your posts
- Sticky sidebar ads: visible as readers scroll
- Header leaderboard: above your navigation

**Add ads.txt:** Go to your AdSense account → Account → ads.txt, then add the provided line to a text file at `yourdomain.com/ads.txt`. This is required to prevent unauthorized selling of your ad inventory.

**Set up payment threshold:** The default payment threshold is $100. You will receive payment once your account balance crosses this amount. It can take 3-6 months of growing traffic to hit this on a new site.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do I need a custom domain?** Technically no — AdSense works on some subdomain hosts — but it is strongly recommended. A custom domain looks more professional and is easier to verify.

**How many page views do I need?** No official minimum. But sites with fewer than 500 monthly page views rarely earn meaningful AdSense revenue. Focus on content first, apply when you have 15+ posts.

**Can I use AdSense and affiliate links on the same site?** Yes. Many successful blogs use both — AdSense for passive display revenue, affiliate links for higher-earning review and comparison content.

**What if I was rejected? Can I reapply?** Yes. Fix the issues, wait at least 30 days, and reapply. There is no limit on reapplications.

## The bottom line

AdSense approval comes down to one thing: does your site look like a real, useful publication? Build it like you are proud of it, not like you are trying to pass a checklist. Sites built for readers get approved; sites built to game the review process usually do not.
